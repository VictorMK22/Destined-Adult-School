Conjunctions

While we were waiting for the bus, an old man came and joined us although we did not know him.

Coordinating Conjunctions

A coordinating conjunction such as and, or, and but joins clauses that are equal in importance together to form compound sentences.

We use and to show addition, e.g., James and John are missing.

We use but to express contrast, e.g., He is clever but arrogant.

We use or to show a choice or option, e.g., Tell James or Carol to come.

Correlative Conjunctions

These conjunctions join clauses of equal importance. They usually occur in pairs and both receive the same attention.

  • Both...and... e.g., Both Muli and Musemi sell clothes.
  • Not only...but also... She was not only stupid but also a stammer.
  • Either...or... e.g., We were to either kill ourselves or be killed by them.
  • Whether...or... Sabina doesn’t know whether she will be admitted to the University of Nairobi or Maseno.
  • Neither...nor... e.g., She is neither beautiful nor hardworking.
  • Hardly/seldom/rarely/barely/scarcely...when... e.g., It was hardly one o’clock when she arrived.
  • Too..to e.g., It was too good a deal for him to be true.
  • So...that... He ran so hard that he fainted on completion.

Exercise

Combine the following sentences using an appropriate correlative conjunction.

  1. Sarah was devoted to her family.
    Sarah was kind to her neighbors.
  2. The boys lost their match.
    The girls lost their match.
  3. We can go on holiday.
    We can do community work.
  4. We don’t know if tomorrow will be sunny.
    We don’t know if tomorrow will be wet.
  5. Kerendi refused to sing.
    Karendi refused to say a word.
  6. She was young.
    She wanted to be president.
  7. It was noon.
    She died.
  8. It was good.
    It was not true.

Subordinating Conjunctions

A subordinating conjunction joins a main clause within a sentence to a subordinate clause or dependent clause.

A dependent clause is a clause within a sentence that cannot make sense on its own. It therefore depends on the independent or main clause for meaning.

The most common subordinating conjunctions are as, because, for, since, whereas, after, before, until, while, as if, as though, except, if, otherwise, unless, although, etc.

Sometimes dependent clauses appear at the end of sentences and other times at the beginning. E.g., Since it is getting late, we have to walk faster.
