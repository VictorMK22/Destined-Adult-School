Auxiliaries

Primary Auxiliaries

Auxiliary verbs are helping verbs. They help the main verbs in a sentence to show tense and mood. E.g.

  • I am cooking food. (Present tense)
  • I had gone home. (Past perfect tense)
  • He must go home. (Imperative mood)

The following primary auxiliaries act as helping verbs:

  • Forms of be: am, is, are, was, were, being
  • Forms of have: has, have, had
  • Forms of do: do, does, did, done

Auxiliary verbs can be used to form questions e.g.

  • Have you seen him?
  • Did she travel?

Auxiliaries do, did, done, and does are used for emphasis e.g.

  • I did win the encounter.
  • I do like him.
  • She does cry a lot.

Sometimes primary auxiliaries act as main verbs e.g.

  • She has a book.
  • She had a knife.
  • John is a good boy.
  • The Wafulas are rich.

Modal Auxiliaries

Modal auxiliaries are verbs which help other verbs to express mood in a sentence which include doubt, certainty, possibility, permission etc.

Mood is usually in the form of:

  1. Ability—could and can e.g. I can swim. We could sing back then.
  2. Requests—can I, shall we, may I... e.g. Can I use your pen? May I talk to your dad? Shall we go.
  3. Permission—you can, you may, you could... e.g. You can go home. You may see the doctor now.
  4. Willingness—can, could, and will e.g. I can give you the money tomorrow. I could steal. I will come.
  5. Wish—may e.g. May God bless you.
  6. Obligation or duty—must, ought to, should e.g. I must finish this work before I go. You ought to pay fees for your children. You should respect your parents.
  7. Certainty—will e.g. I will come next week. We will all die.
  8. Possibility or probability—can, might, may, could e.g. I can win the race. He might come. She may recover. We could lose.
  9. Reassuring—will e.g. it will be OK.
  10. Threatening—will e.g. I will kill you.
